Navigating Your CSE Meeting in District 21

Committee on Special Education (CSE) meetings can feel overwhelming. Understanding the process and knowing what questions to ask is crucial for advocating for your child. In District 21, which includes communities like Coney Island, Gravesend, and Brighton Beach, it's vital to address specific needs related to bilingual evaluations, specialized programs, and community access.

Who's at the Table? Expect a team. The most important people are you, the parent/guardian, and the CSE Chairperson, who guides the meeting and ensures compliance. Also present will be a General Education Teacher, a Special Education Teacher, a school psychologist and importantly, a Parent Member. The Parent Member is another parent of a child with special needs, there to support YOU. Related service providers (speech therapist, occupational therapist, etc.) may attend based on your child's needs.

District 21 Focus: Given the large Russian-speaking population, remember to inquire about the availability and quality of Russian-speaking bilingual evaluations to ensure your child's needs are accurately assessed. If considering a D75 program, ask about the program's specifics: student-to-teacher ratios, specialized therapies offered, and transition planning. Finally, for families in our beach communities, don't hesitate to bring up accessibility concerns related to school events and activities near the waterfront.

Questions to Ask: Come prepared! Here are three questions tailored for District 21 families:

  • "Can you describe the process for obtaining a Russian-speaking bilingual evaluation and who will be conducting the evaluation?"
  • "What are the specific D75 program options available in District 21, and how do these programs address individual learning styles and needs?"
  • "How does the school ensure accessibility for students with disabilities at events held near the beach or other community locations?"

Remember, you are your child's best advocate. Be prepared, be informed, and don't hesitate to ask questions.
